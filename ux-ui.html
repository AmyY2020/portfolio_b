2020, this is a very challenging year. Online learning became more serious for all students including children. From the beginning of May, I started my first UX project, lasting one and a half month from research to iteration and finished a high-fidelity design. I hope the application can play the role of tutors and help children overcome the challenges and make the studying process more effective.

CHALLENGES

One big challenge of my design is that the buyers (parents or guardians) are not the users (children), so I need to concern about their expectations to make the design more accessible and motivate users to keep learning.

MY PRIMARY RESEARCH

Understand the users’Behaviours, Motivation, and Pains will help me identify the potential problems.

FINDING / INSIGHTS

  • Task-taken can motivate children to keep learning.

  • Handwriting is valued by parents or guardians.

  • Children play longer in a concise interface with clear guidance.

IDEATION

The combinatorics method can help a designer clarify the product's main functionalities; for example:

It is like a tutor;

Without (do not have to) meet in person,

(Plus) give tutoring based on in dividual needs.

The possible features: 

  • Design learning and trying page to identify the knowledge level

  • Support fingers or e-pen to interact

  • Use AI to analyze the result of quiz and provide suggestion

START WITH SKETCH
TEST I: a low-fidelity test

Purpose

  • If children can realize some key functionalities such as “suggestion”, “ scratching".

  • If children understand the basic function of every page.

Finding

  • The function of scratch and suggestion are not easy being noticed.

  • “Try” feature is attractive.

VERSION I

A version of high fidelity including an iteration based on test 1

  • Design the pop-up window after the quiz and highlight the “suggestion” bottom to draw an attention. 

  • Change the top of navigation bar to make the navigation clearer. (it was changed back after the further test)

  • Consider an instruction for new users

  • Provide “hint” to improve the “try” feature

VERSION II: accessibility consideration

  • Add reading function for user to choose listening the question

  • Allow users to choose how to interact with the interface, such as clicking and scratching using the setting menu

TEST 2

Used ‘lookback’ to collect the feedback.

Goal:

  • Smoothly go through all pages

  • Identify the location successfully

  • Understand the function of every page

  • Finish the task without confusing

  • Answer the question of math

  • Fix the error

  • Use “hint” to solve problems

  • A tendency to click “suggestion”

INSIGHTS

  • If there is not another option or when users are confused, some images may give them an illusion that is clickable. 

  • The expected clickable button should be highlighted, and the buttons should be close to its related contents. 

  • The position of navigation bar could be a destruction when children go through a task.

  • It is hard for someone to fix errors by clicking the same place again, especially for kids.

  • There is a sign that the popup window may interrupt children when they go through a task. 

FINAL VERSION

  • Change the top navigation to reduce interruption

  • Change “result page” to give users a shortcut to access their historical recording

  • Move “try”, “quiz”, “suggestion” page to a single layer to increase children’s focus on problem-solving

  • Use “try again” button to refresh the error page

  • Add “print” icon in “suggestion sheets” to allow user to download to practice

What I learned:

As the first UX case study, a reflection will help me prepare for further learning by recording the successes and failures. Through a completed process: research, design, test, and iteration, I gained hands-on experience to understand the role of UX and to practice the tech skills by using related tools such as Figma, Miro, LookBack, and Zeplin.

During the research stage, an observation was the most valuable way to understand children’s behaviors of using a digital product. To parents or guardians, asking children’s learning routine was relatively objective to understand their attitude toward learning and expectation.

I used three approaches to test my design: Zoom, lookback, and in-person. The most surprising feedback from the first test was that the images gave people a clickable illusion. Due to the challenge of inviting more participants, I did not get more insightful results. However, the reward is to understand the entire design process, and it helped me start my first step of UX.
